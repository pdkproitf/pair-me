# Domain notes: News / Current Events

For breaking stories, ongoing situations, public figures, or "what's actually
happening/happened with X." Layer this on top of the universal criteria in SKILL.md.
This domain moves fast and is where misinformation risk is highest — be extra
careful about verification here.

## What "authoritative" means here

Ranked roughly highest to lowest trust:

1. **Primary documents and statements** — official transcripts, government
   releases, court filings, the organization's or person's own statement. Always
   worth finding even if you also cite reporting about them.
2. **Wire services** (AP, Reuters, AFP) — the industry's fact-checking baseline;
   most other outlets license and republish their reporting.
3. **Outlets with named reporters, a masthead, and a correction policy**, ideally
   with a specific beat covering this story (not a general aggregator). Check the
   byline is a real, identifiable reporter, not "Staff" with no track record.
4. **Multiple independent outlets confirming the same fact independently** — this
   is the single strongest signal in this domain. If five articles cite the same
   one original tweet or press release, that's one source with five reprints, not
   five sources.
5. **Fact-checking organizations** (with transparent methodology) for disputed
   claims specifically.
6. **Aggregators, social media posts, and unverified eyewitness accounts** — can be
   the earliest signal of a breaking story, but rank low until corroborated by the
   above; explicitly flag as unverified if included.

## What to search for

- The specific event + exact date, to pin down the timeline before anything else
- `"[topic]" site:apnews.com OR site:reuters.com` for wire-service baseline
- The named officials, agencies, or organizations involved, directly (their own
  statements/press pages)
- `"[claim]" fact check` for anything that reads like it could be contested or viral
- The story a few days *after* the initial break — early reporting is often
  revised; later pieces tend to be more accurate and complete

## Domain-specific red flags

- **Single-sourced claims presented as fact**, especially anything attributed to
  "sources say" or "sources familiar with the matter" with no corroboration.
- **Outlets with a clear, undisclosed institutional or political agenda** presenting
  opinion or advocacy as straight news — note the outlet's known lean if relevant
  and prefer straight reporting when available.
- **Satire or clearly opinion/op-ed content** mistaken for reporting — check the
  section/label.
- **AI-generated or hastily rewritten aggregator content** with no original
  reporting, common on low-effort "news" sites that exist purely for ad clicks.
- **Screenshots and social posts without links to primary source** — easy to fake
  or misattribute; don't rank these as verified.
- **Stale "breaking news" pages** that were accurate at time of posting but never
  updated as the story developed — check the last-updated timestamp.

## What to weigh more heavily than usual

- Independent corroboration across outlets with no shared ownership or wire feed.
- Recency and update history — for an evolving story, the freshest well-sourced
  piece usually beats an earlier one, even if the earlier one broke the story first.
- Whether the outlet has since issued corrections on this specific story — check if
  contested details have been walked back.
