# Domain notes: Business

For topics about companies, markets, industries, deals, competitive dynamics, or
business strategy. Layer this on top of the universal criteria in SKILL.md.

## What "authoritative" means here

Ranked roughly highest to lowest trust:

1. **Primary company disclosures** — SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, S-1), investor-
   relations pages, earnings call transcripts, official press releases. These are
   legally accountable statements, not marketing copy, even though they're written
   by the company.
2. **Regulatory and government data** — antitrust filings, court documents, census/
   labor statistics, central bank data, patent filings.
3. **Established financial/business press with named reporters** — outlets with a
   track record of correction policies and beat reporters who cover the specific
   industry (not just wire-service reprints of a press release).
4. **Analyst and research-firm reports** — sell-side equity research, Gartner/
   Forrester-style industry reports, credit-rating agency notes. High value but
   note potential conflicts (a bank's analyst covering a client, a vendor-funded
   "independent" report).
5. **Trade publications** — narrower but often more technically precise than
   general business press for a specific industry.
6. **Independent analysis / substacks / blogs by recognized practitioners** — value
   depends entirely on the specific author's track record; verify who they are and
   whether their past calls held up.

## What to search for

- `"[company] 10-K"` or `"[company] investor relations"` for primary disclosures
- `"[company] earnings call transcript"` for management's own framing and analyst
  Q&A
- `"[topic] market size" "market research"` for sizing, but treat single-source
  market-size numbers skeptically — corroborate across two independent estimates
- `"[company/topic]" site:sec.gov` for US-listed companies
- The specific beat reporter's name once found, to find their other coverage
- `"[topic] antitrust"` / `"[topic] lawsuit"` for the adversarial view competitors
  and regulators have surfaced

## Domain-specific red flags

- **Press releases treated as independent reporting.** A press release describes
  what a company wants said, not necessarily what's true — rank it lower than
  independent coverage that verifies the claims.
- **Sponsored/native content** labeled subtly as "Partner Content" or "Brand
  Studio" on otherwise reputable outlets.
- **Pump-focused content**: stock-promotion sites, "10 stocks to buy now" pieces,
  anything whose real business model is ad revenue from trading platforms.
- **Stale market-size or funding numbers** re-cited for years without updating —
  common in "market size" stats that get copy-pasted across hundreds of SEO pages
  from one original (often shaky) estimate.
- **Single-source claims presented as consensus.** If every article traces back to
  the same original report, that's one source, not corroboration — say so.

## What to weigh more heavily than usual

- Cross-corroboration between an independent source and the company's own filing.
- Whether financial figures are audited/filed vs. self-reported in a press release.
- Recency matters a lot for anything about current market position, valuation, or
  leadership — a 2023 "market leader" claim may already be stale.
